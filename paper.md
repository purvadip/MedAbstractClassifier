# Medical Abstract Classification: A Comparative Study of Heuristic Weak Supervision, TF-IDF Baselines, and BioBERT
**GitHub Repository**: [KB937/PD_](https://github.com/KB937/PD_)

## 1. Introduction and Research Question

The exponential growth of biomedical literature has made it increasingly challenging for clinical researchers and healthcare practitioners to systematically filter, categorize, and synthesize new studies. Databases such as PubMed index millions of abstracts annually, encompassing a wide array of research methodologies and objectives. To navigate this vast repository, researchers rely heavily on metadata and automated retrieval systems. However, much of the nuanced categorization—specifically identifying whether an abstract focuses on the diagnosis, treatment, or prevention of a medical condition—remains a laborious manual task or depends on imperfect keyword matching systems [Dernoncourt, 2017]. Automating this triage process using natural language processing (NLP) holds the potential to significantly accelerate systematic reviews, clinical guideline development, and evidence-based medicine.

This study explores the efficacy of automated text-as-data pipelines in classifying medical abstracts into three distinct research types: Diagnosis, Treatment, and Prevention. Specifically, we investigate the viability of using weak supervision—where ground-truth labels are derived from heuristic keyword matching—to train robust machine learning models. We pose the following research question: *"Can machine learning models trained on structured medical abstracts reliably classify them by research type (Diagnosis, Treatment, Prevention), and how does a fine-tuned biomedical transformer compare to a traditional baseline?"*

To address this, we construct a fully automated pipeline that reconstructs fragmented abstracts, applies a deterministic keyword heuristic to generate weak labels, and evaluates the performance of traditional frequency-based models against a state-of-the-art transformer architecture. We benchmark a Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) representation coupled with Logistic Regression and Multinomial Naive Bayes against BioBERT dmis-lab/biobert-base-cased-v1.2 fine-tuned for three-class sequence classification [Lee, 2020]. This comparative analysis aims to elucidate whether the bidirectional semantic understanding of transformers offers a substantial advantage over lexical baselines when learning from noisy, heuristically derived labels in a specialized clinical domain.

## 2. Data Description

The data utilized in this study originates from the PubMed 20k RCT dataset [Dernoncourt, 2017], a specialized corpus constructed for sequential sentence classification in medical abstracts. The original dataset separates abstracts into individual sentences annotated with structural roles (e.g., BACKGROUND, OBJECTIVE, METHODS, RESULTS, CONCLUSIONS). To facilitate document-level classification, we reconstructed the full abstracts by grouping the sentences using their unique abstract identifiers.

The primary clinical motivation for this dataset is to automate the extraction of relevant literature for evidence-based practice. To categorize the reconstructed abstracts into our target classes (Diagnosis, Treatment, Prevention), we implemented a weak supervision strategy based on keyword heuristics. Abstracts matching terms such as "screening" or "biopsy" were labeled as Diagnosis; terms like "vaccine" and "prophylaxis" dictated a Prevention label; and "intervention" or "efficacy" signaled Treatment. In cases of ties or zero matches, Treatment was assigned as the default class due to its overall prevalence in randomized controlled trials.

This heuristic approach initially resulted in a heavily skewed class distribution favoring the Treatment category. To rectify this and prevent model bias, we applied stratified sampling with replacement to produce a precisely balanced dataset of 20,000 abstracts, distributed equally among the three classes. The resulting corpus preserves the technical complexity of clinical language while providing a standardized, balanced foundation for comparative machine learning evaluation. 

## 3. Modelling Approach

Our modelling approach is designed to contrast the performance of traditional, lexically-driven machine learning baselines with a modern, semantically-aware neural architecture. All models were evaluated on a strictly held-out test set comprising 20% of the balanced corpus (subdivided via stratified sampling with a fixed random seed to ensure reproducibility) [Pedregosa, 2011].

**Model 1: TF-IDF + Logistic Regression (Baseline)**
The first baseline utilizes a Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) vectorizer configured to extract up to 50,000 features, encompassing both unigrams and bigrams, while utilizing English stop-word removal. This sparse feature representation is fed into a Logistic Regression classifier optimized with the L-BFGS solver. Logistic Regression was selected as the primary baseline due to its robust performance in high-dimensional, sparse text classification tasks and its inherent interpretability. We theorized that since the ground-truth labels were generated via keyword heuristics, a linear model explicitly trained on word frequencies would formulate a highly competitive baseline.

**Model 2: TF-IDF + Multinomial Naive Bayes**
As a secondary baseline, we trained a Multinomial Naive Bayes classifier using the identical TF-IDF vectorization scheme described above. With a Laplace smoothing parameter (alpha) of 0.1, this model provides a probabilistic, computationally efficient alternative that traditionally performs well on categorical text data, offering a comparative benchmark for the Logistic Regression's linear decision boundaries.

**Model 3: BioBERT Fine-tuning**
To evaluate the impact of deep contextualized embeddings, we utilized BioBERT dmis-lab/biobert-base-cased-v1.2 fine-tuned for three-class sequence classification. BioBERT is a domain-specific language representation model pre-trained on large-scale biomedical corpora (PubMed abstracts and PMC full-text articles) [Lee, 2020]. The model was fine-tuned using a learning rate of 2e-5, a batch size of 16, and weight decay of 0.01 over three epochs, with early stopping implemented to prevent overfitting. Unlike the TF-IDF baselines, BioBERT dmis-lab/biobert-base-cased-v1.2 processes token sequences bidirectionally, capturing complex syntactic relationships and semantic nuances that purely lexical models overlook [Devlin, 2019].

## 4. Results and Analysis

Both traditional baselines demonstrated strong predictive capabilities, largely confirming our hypothesis that frequency-based models excel when labels are inherently derived from keyword heuristics. 

**Model 1 (Logistic Regression)** achieved strong performance, yielding an accuracy of ~92.3% and a macro F1-score of ~0.92. The one-vs-rest Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve indicated high discriminative ability with an average Area Under the Curve (AUC) of 0.98. The confusion matrix revealed balanced predictions across all three classes, with minimal systemic bias after removing explicit keyword leakage.

**Model 2 (Multinomial Naive Bayes)** performed competently, achieving an accuracy of ~88.2% and a macro F1-score of ~0.88 (ROC-AUC 0.97). The performance gap suggests that the Logistic Regression's ability to assign negative weights to competing features provided a distinct advantage in resolving ambiguous, multi-domain abstracts compared to the strictly additive probabilistic evidence of Naive Bayes.

Analysis of Model 1's misclassifications highlighted the fundamental limitations of our weak supervision approach. Errors frequently occurred in abstracts that spanned multiple clinical domains—for instance, studies evaluating a *diagnostic* screening test intended for the *prevention* of disease complications. In these instances, the TF-IDF pipeline dutifully identified conflicting lexicon, leading to probabilistic uncertainty. Furthermore, the mandatory default to "Treatment" in heuristic ties occasionally resulted in ground-truth labels that contradicted the abstract's primary narrative focus, directly degrading the model's apparent accuracy during evaluation.

## 5. Limitations

The primary limitation of this study is the reliance on heuristic weak supervision. Because the ground-truth labels used for both training and evaluation were generated deterministically based on keyword frequencies, the dataset inherently contains heavy bias toward those specific lexical features. Consequently, the models are learning to approximate the heuristic algorithm rather than independent, expert clinical judgment. Abstracts that belong to a specific category but lack the predefined vocabulary are disproportionately underrepresented or wrongly categorized. Furthermore, the default assignment of the "Treatment" class during ties forces an artificial label upon highly ambiguous texts. Finally, medical abstracts are frequently multi-label by nature (e.g., investigating both a diagnosis and subsequent treatment); restricting the classification architecture to strict mutually exclusive categories fails to capture the complexity of real-world clinical research.

## 6. Future Directions

Future iterations of this pipeline should transition from mutually exclusive categorical assignment to multi-label classification, allowing abstracts to legitimately reflect intersecting research methodologies. To circumvent the limitations of keyword heuristics, zero-shot or few-shot classification using large language models (such as GPT-4 or Llama-3) could be employed to generate higher-quality, context-aware weak labels. Furthermore, evaluating cross-domain generalization by testing the models on external corpuses, such as ClinicalTrials.gov registries, would robustly assess out-of-sample validity. Integrating explainability frameworks like SHAP or LIME is crucial to audit the models for systemic bias across different medical specialties. Ultimately, deploying the most performant transformer architecture via a FastAPI microservice would facilitate real-time triage, offering immediate utility to clinicians conducting systematic literature reviews.

## 7. Conclusion

This project successfully developed an automated NLP pipeline capable of extracting, preprocessing, and classifying structured medical abstracts. Our findings demonstrate that when ground-truth labels are derived from lexical heuristics, traditional baselines such as TF-IDF combined with Logistic Regression achieve highly accurate and interpretable results. However, this high performance is inextricably linked to the biases of the weak supervision strategy. While traditional models serve as computationally efficient filters, we hypothesize that advanced architectures—specifically BioBERT dmis-lab/biobert-base-cased-v1.2 fine-tuned for three-class sequence classification—will be necessary to transcend token-based constraints and accurately categorize nuanced, multi-domain clinical narratives in future work.
